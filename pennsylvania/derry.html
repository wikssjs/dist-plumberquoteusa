What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($992-$2977) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most Derry families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our Derry plumbers can advise based on your usage.

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by (or under the direct supervision of) a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the Pennsylvania Department of Labor & Industry, and you can verify credentials via dli.pa.gov (and related listings from the PA Plumbing Board). Before work begins in Derry or anywhere in Dauphin County, it’s reasonable to ask for the plumber’s license number and to confirm that the right licensed professional is on the job.

Permits are typically required for work that changes systems or requires inspection. In most cases, the following DOES require a permit: new installation of any plumbing fixture, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work (including repairs that require excavation or inspection), and any work that a local code officer requires inspected. Repairs like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or swapping a supply line for a straightforward fixture often do not require a permit—because they’re usually minor repairs rather than system-level changes.

Two additional compliance points matter to homeowners. First, lead service lines: PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral from street to house may still be lead in older Derry homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so the work is coordinated correctly. Second, workers’ compensation: verify coverage via WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov) and request a certificate of insurance showing your property address. Homeowners can face liability for injured uninsured workers on their property.

To verify step-by-step, check the plumber’s license on the PA registry, request a certificate of insurance that lists your address, confirm workers’ comp coverage through WCAIS, and ensure the job-specific permits are planned before the first cut is made.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Derry homeowners

With about 53.9% of Derry’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, many homes still have galvanized supply lines (common in older construction) and cast iron drains that are approaching the end of their reliable service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, which can reduce water pressure and eventually cause leaks at threaded joints, couplings, and natural weak points. Meanwhile, older cast iron drain lines may develop cracks or are pulled into failure by root intrusion and joint deterioration.

The decision between targeted repair, partial repipe, or a full repipe depends on what’s failing and how widespread the problem is. If the leak is isolated and accessible, spot repair often makes sense. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (especially where galvanized threads meet), it’s often a sign that the interior wall loss and corrosion pattern are already underway. Climate matters too: Pennsylvania’s freeze–thaw cycles stress aging plumbing, and a system that was “working” until a cold snap may fail more quickly after.

Here’s a practical example: if you have one accessible leak and the rest of the supply shows good pressure, a spot repair may come in around $350–$2,500. If you’re instead dealing with repeated leaks at multiple fixtures or the main supply, a partial repipe is more likely to cost meaningfully more—often in the $1,100–$950 band listed for partial work—but it can be justified because it stops you from paying for repeated emergency fixes. And if the home has pre-1960 galvanized supply in hard-to-replace runs, full repiping can be the safer long-term choice.

How to choose a plumbing contractor in Derry

Choosing the right plumber in Derry starts with credential checks and ends with a clear, written scope. First, verify licensing through Pennsylvania’s online registry (the PA Department of Labor & Industry listings). If the company offers certifications like IICRC (for water damage restoration-related work), ask for proof of current certification and where it applies; you can generally confirm status through the certifying body’s verification tools. Don’t accept “we’re licensed” as a blanket statement—ask for the license number and the individual’s role on your job.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes. Make sure each quote breaks out labor and materials rather than giving only a lump sum. For example, a water heater replacement quote should list the unit type, venting components, supply lines, any required hookups, and haul-away. Scope matters: ask what’s excluded (drywall restoration, camera inspection, permit fees, disposal, parts if discontinued). If you’re filing an insurance claim after a leak, confirm the contractor will provide documentation (photos, notes, measurements) that supports the claim.

Warranty should be in writing. Ask about workmanship coverage length and whether equipment/materials include manufacturer warranties. For payment schedule, never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back until the job is complete and tested. Finally, timeline should be explicit: request an arrival estimate and a completion window in writing, even for “standard” work.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water supply lines coated to resist corrosion when they were installed. Over time—especially in older PA homes—galvanized tubing corrodes from the inside out, which can lead to rusty water, reduced flow, and leaks at threaded joints. In Derry, where a substantial portion of homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), galvanized supply is something I routinely inspect when homeowners complain about inconsistent pressure, mineral buildup, or recurring pinhole leaks.

Should you replace them? If you’ve had a single leak in an accessible section, spot repair may be reasonable. But if you have recurring leaks or pressure drop throughout the house, replacement is often the best long-term decision—sometimes starting with a partial repipe and, in worse cases, moving toward a full repipe to copper or PEX. If you’re weighing costs, planned work typically costs less than emergency repairs; and an emergency burst-pipe call in the $2,500–$3,500 band is often exactly what prompts homeowners to stop chasing leaks.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Derry winters?

Frozen pipes are a real risk in Pennsylvania because winter temperatures swing and freeze–thaw cycles can stress older plumbing. Prevention starts with insulation and heat management. Insulate exposed supply lines in unheated basements, crawlspaces, and along exterior walls, and seal drafts around where pipes enter the house. If you have shutoffs on exterior walls or in uninsulated mechanical areas, keep them accessible and protected.

During cold snaps, keep a steady trickle at faucets served by vulnerable lines, especially if you’ve had past freezing events. Open cabinet doors under sinks to let warm air circulate. If you’ve got a history of galvanized supply line leaks (common in pre-1980 housing stock), pay extra attention to connection points where corrosion and joints add weak spots.

If you do experience active freezing or a leak, treat it as an emergency: a burst pipe repair can run from $350–$2,500 for standard response and into $2,500–$3,500 when it’s active and after-hours. A good prevention plan is usually cheaper than emergency restoration.
