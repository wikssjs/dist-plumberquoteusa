What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($934-$2803) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most New Kensington families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our New Kensington plumbers can advise based on your usage.

Signs you may need repiping in New Kensington: low water pressure throughout the home (not just one fixture), discolored or rusty water, frequent leaks in different locations, visible corrosion on pipes, and homes with original galvanized steel pipes (common in PA homes built before 1970). Full-home repiping in copper or PEX runs $622-$2076 depending on home size. A licensed New Kensington plumber can inspect and advise — often a camera inspection will confirm if repiping is needed.
How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?
In New Kensington, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Hard water in many PA areas accelerates scale buildup and reduces lifespan — annual flushing and an anode rod check extends tank life by 2-4 years. When your heater is over 10 years old and shows signs of rust, inconsistent heating, or visible corrosion, replacement at $934-$2803 is usually smarter than repair.

A standard water heater replacement in New Kensington by a licensed plumber typically takes 2-4 hours, including draining and removing the old unit, installing the new heater, connecting supply lines, relighting pilots (gas), and testing. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued by the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify status at dli.pa.gov or through the PA Plumbing Board resources.

Permits are typically required for work that changes plumbing systems enough to warrant inspection, including: new installation of any fixture, replacing plumbing pipe beyond minor repairs, water heater installation/replacement, sewer line work, and any job that will tie into or modify systems that local code officials inspect. Repairs like fixing a leaking faucet or replacing a toilet flapper typically do not require a permit because they don’t alter the plumbing system in a major way.

For lead service lines: PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from street to house) can still be lead in older New Kensington homes (especially pre-1986). Ask your plumber to test and document what material you have, and coordinate with the utility on replacement responsibilities.

Workers’ compensation matters too. Verify coverage via WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov, and request a certificate of insurance listing your property address—without proper coverage, homeowners can face liability if a worker is injured on the jobsite.

To verify credentials step-by-step in New Kensington: (1) confirm the plumber’s license number online through DLI/Plumbing Board tools, (2) ask for a current certificate of insurance, (3) verify workers’ compensation coverage in WCAIS, and (4) confirm who will pull permits before the work begins.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for New Kensington homeowners

With about 91.1% of New Kensington’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, many Westmoreland County homes still have galvanized steel supply lines (common in homes built before 1960) or cast iron drain lines nearing the end of their service life. The best decision between targeted repair and partial or full repipe depends on system age, how frequently leaks recur, water pressure trends, water quality concerns, and how much of the piping is accessible without major demolition. If leaks are isolated to one reachable section, repair is often the most cost-effective option. If you’re seeing recurring leaks (especially on galvanized runs) or low pressure throughout the house, it usually signals a systemic corrosion problem that spot fixes can’t fix permanently.

Here’s a practical example: if you’re paying for repeated leak calls, a single spot repair may fall in the $350–$1,800 range when the pipe is accessible. But if you’re paying for multiple repairs over a short period, it can become more economical to plan a partial repipe or full replacement—particularly when the alternative is ongoing water damage restoration. That’s why, in older homes near the downtown and in older basement layouts, I often recommend diagnosis (pressure tests and pipe probing) before you keep chasing leaks.

What causes low water pressure in New Kensington homes?

Low water pressure in New Kensington is commonly tied to older piping and mineral or corrosion buildup—especially in homes built before 1980. When galvanized steel supplies corrode over decades, the inside diameter reduces and water flow suffers. In some cases, pressure is normal at the meter or shutoff but drops after water moves through older runs inside the home, which points toward a pipe-material problem rather than a utility issue.

Another contributor can be partial blockages at fixtures, shutoff valves, or aerators. However, if the pressure is low throughout multiple bathrooms and the kitchen, it’s more likely the supply system is aging. Westmoreland County’s freeze–thaw cycles can also stress older joints and create intermittent flow restrictions, especially after prior leak repairs where connections may not be fully aligned or where older sections remain.

If your plumber determines the cause is galvanized corrosion, the repair path can involve diagnosis and repipe. Depending on how much piping needs replacement and how much access is required, you may see estimates in the mid-to-higher range associated with repiping. In many New Kensington homes, crews attempt to target the worst sections first, but widespread pressure loss often pushes toward a broader approach.

The most reliable step is to test pressure at key points and inspect valve condition before deciding on repairs.
