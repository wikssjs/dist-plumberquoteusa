Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work generally must be performed by—or directly supervised by—a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry (verify licensing at dli.pa.gov) and the relevant plumbing board resources. This matters because plumbing failures can create property damage and safety hazards, and permits ensure the installation meets current code.

Permits are typically required for: new installation of any fixture, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and any work that requires inspection by the local code officer. Repairs like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or swapping a supply line washer are usually considered minor and often do not require a permit—however, your plumber should confirm based on the exact scope.

Lead service lines are also a real consideration in older Glenolden homes. Pennsylvania Act 2021-41 addresses lead service line replacement by water utilities, but your homeowner-side lateral (from the street to your house) may still contain lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so the right side of the line is handled.

Before work begins, confirm workers’ compensation coverage using the WCAIS verification portal at wcais.pa.gov. Also request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. If a worker is injured on your property and the contractor is uninsured or underinsured, homeowners can face liability.

To verify a contractor step-by-step: (1) request license numbers and check them on the state registry, (2) confirm the certificate of insurance matches your address and includes proper coverage, and (3) verify workers’ compensation through WCAIS or via a documented WC acceptance/coverage proof.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Glenolden homeowners

With 87% of Glenolden’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, it’s common to find galvanized steel supply lines (especially in older homes) and cast-iron drain lines that are nearing the end of their service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, often first showing up as reduced pressure, then leaks at threaded joints, and eventually sudden failures. Cast iron waste lines can last a long time, but once cracks form or roots intrude, the system can clog repeatedly and backups become more likely.

The decision between targeted repair and partial or full repipe comes down to system age, the pattern of failures, water quality concerns, and how much piping is accessible. In Delaware County’s older blocks, partial repairs sometimes become “whack-a-mole” because adjacent sections are at the same corrosion stage. That’s why a single leak doesn’t always justify repiping, but recurring leaks often do.

Here’s a local way to think about it: if you’re paying for emergency stopgaps multiple times, you may end up spending nearly the cost of a more durable solution anyway. For example, if a recurring galvanized leak turns into repeated repairs that each land in the $300–$4,500 range—plus disruption and restoration—the economics can swing toward a partial repipe. Conversely, if an accessible line section fails once and the rest of the system shows good pressure and no other leaks, spot repair is usually the smarter, lower-cost move.

What causes low water pressure in Glenolden homes?

Low water pressure in Glenolden is often tied to the plumbing materials and age of the system. What you’ll pay depends on what the plumber finds. What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply lines coated with zinc. In many older Pennsylvania homes (including areas of Delaware County with pre-1980 construction), galvanized piping is common. Over decades, the coating and steel can corrode internally. That corrosion reduces water pressure, makes flow inconsistent, and increases the risk of leaks at joints and threaded connections. In freeze-thaw conditions, weakened sections can be more vulnerable in winter months.

Should you replace them? Often, the answer depends on how the system behaves. If you have one small, accessible leak and no other pressure problems, spot repair may be reasonable. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks or widespread pressure loss, replacement becomes more cost-effective because more of the line is likely at the same corrosion stage.

Cost-wise, Glenolden homeowners typically compare the repair pattern against repipe budgets. If emergency repairs are frequently landing in the broader $300–$4,500 range (not counting disruption/restoration), repeated calls can outweigh the value of upgrading to modern piping like copper or PEX. A licensed plumber should inspect where galvanized exists, pressure test if appropriate, and recommend spot vs partial vs full repipe based on system condition—not just the first leak they find.
