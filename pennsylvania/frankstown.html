Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licences are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry, and you can confirm status at dli.pa.gov or via the PA Plumbing Board. For Frankstown homeowners, permits aren’t just paperwork—they’re often the reason a “simple replacement” turns into code-compliance upgrades once the project is reviewed.

Permits are typically required for work such as: installation of new fixtures; replacing plumbing piping beyond minor repairs; water heater installation (especially when venting, gas connections, electrical components, or structural changes are involved); sewer line work (spot repair, relining, or excavation); and any work that will be inspected by the local code officer. In contrast, repairs like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or minor clogs generally do not require a permit.

Two other compliance points matter in Blair County homes. First, lead service lines: PA Act 2021-41 focuses on utility replacement, but the homeowner-side lateral (from street to house) may still contain lead in pre-1986 properties. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material before replacement decisions. Second, workers’ compensation: verify coverage using wcais.pa.gov and request a certificate of insurance showing your property address—because homeowners can be exposed to liability if uninsured workers are injured on-site.

Step-by-step: (1) ask for the plumber’s licence number, then verify on the PA licensing registry; (2) request a certificate of insurance listing your address; (3) confirm workers’ compensation coverage through WCAIS; and (4) ask whether permits are required for your specific scope, then confirm the permit is being pulled before work begins.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Frankstown homeowners

With 55% of Frankstown’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, many Blair County homes still have piping systems that are showing their age. Galvanized steel supply lines—common in older construction—corrode internally over time, reducing flow and eventually leading to leaks. Cast iron drain lines may be long-lived, but when they show cracking, scaling, or root intrusion, they can become a recurring maintenance problem rather than a one-time fix. In Frankstown, the repipe decision is also affected by access limitations: many older basements and crawlspaces make “targeted” repairs more disruptive if the plumber can’t reach the damaged section without opening finishes.

In practice, targeted repair is best when the failure is isolated and accessible, while partial or full repipe makes more sense when failures repeat or when multiple sections show corrosion. A good diagnosis often costs less than repeated emergency calls, because it identifies whether the leak is a one-off seam failure or part of a systemic problem across the distribution lines.

Here’s a helpful way to think about it with real dollars. If you have a single leak in an accessible pipe, a spot repair is often in the $350–$1,500 range and avoids major disruption. But if the same galvanized piping type is producing recurring leaks, a partial repipe can be justified because it prevents the “pay a small amount now, pay again later” cycle—particularly when corrosion is already closing off flow passages and increasing risk of another burst during a freeze-thaw swing. For some pre-1960 homes with galvanized supply lines, repipe decisions can become the safer long-term move, but only after assessing how much pipe is actually corroded and what the access situation looks like.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply lines coated with zinc. They were common in many Pennsylvania homes built decades ago, including in properties like those in Frankstown where a significant share of housing stock is older (54.6% built before 1980, U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024). Over time, galvanized steel corrodes from the inside out. That corrosion can narrow the pipe’s internal diameter, which leads to reduced water pressure and increases the risk of leaks and eventual pipe failure.

Whether to replace galvanized pipes depends on condition and symptoms. If you have a single, accessible leak, a spot repair may be reasonable—often in the $350–$1,500 range for standard work. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks, frequent pressure drops, discolored water, or multiple problem zones, replacement (partial or full repipe) is usually the more cost-effective long-term fix. For example, repeated failures on galvanized systems often push homeowners toward partial repipe planning and, in more extensive corrosion situations, full repipe to modern materials like copper or PEX.

A good plumber will also confirm what’s actually galvanized (supply lines versus other sections) and discuss access challenges typical of older Frankstown basements and crawlspaces.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Frankstown winters?

Frankstown winters can be hard on older homes, especially those with uninsulated basements, crawlspaces, and exterior wall runs. Freeze-thaw cycles increase the chance that water inside a pipe expands and cracks at weak seams, leading to burst-pipe emergencies. Prevention starts with keeping water moving and keeping pipes warm enough to avoid freezing.

Practical steps: (1) insulate exposed supply lines in basements and crawlspaces with pipe insulation rated for cold weather; (2) seal air leaks where plumbing passes through exterior walls; (3) let faucets drip slightly during extreme cold so the system doesn’t fully freeze; (4) keep garage or basement doors closer to indoors if attached or semi-finished; and (5) during prolonged cold snaps, open cabinet doors where plumbing runs are located on exterior walls. If you already have areas that freeze, ask a plumber about adding heat tape (installed correctly) or relocating/insulating problem runs.

Because the local housing stock includes many pre-1980 homes, repairs often need diagnosis before the real fix—so prevention is usually cheaper than responding to a burst. If you suspect a frozen section, don’t forcefully thaw with open flame; call for safe thawing and inspection.
