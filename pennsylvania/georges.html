Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed plumber (Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber). Licences are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify status at dli.pa.gov and confirm the contractor’s standing through the PA Plumbing Board processes. The key for Georges homeowners is knowing what typically needs a permit versus what usually does not.

Work that typically DOES require a permit includes new installation of plumbing fixtures, any pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work (including spot repairs that disturb the line), and any job that local officials require to be inspected. If a project involves moving drains, changing venting, or opening walls/floors for new piping, you should expect a permit path in most municipalities under the Uniform Construction Code framework.

Work that typically does NOT require a permit includes fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or straightforward replacement of a supply fitting that doesn’t alter the plumbing system layout.

On lead: Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 targets lead service lines from the utility side, but the homeowner-side lateral from the street to the house can still contain lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material before replacement. Also confirm worker coverage: request a certificate of insurance and verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov. When you hire, check (1) PA license status online, (2) an insurance certificate listing your property address where appropriate, and (3) workers’ compensation proof—these steps protect you if something goes wrong on a tough Fayette County basement job.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Georges homeowners

With 76.7% of Georges-area housing stock dating to pre-1980, many Fayette County homes still have galvanized steel supply lines (common in homes built before 1960) or cast-iron drain lines approaching the end of their service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, often showing up as pinhole leaks, recurring pressure complaints, or water that intermittently turns cloudy after disturbances. Cast iron drains can last a long time, but once cracks, corrosion holes, or root intrusion appear, backups and slow flow often return.

In Georges, the decision between targeted repair and partial or full repipe is usually about pattern and accessibility: how many leaks you’ve had, whether leaks cluster around one type of pipe (galvanized vs copper vs PVC transitions), and how much of the pipe is buried behind finishes. If you’re dealing with a single, accessible failure, spot repair can be cost-effective. But if you’re repeatedly opening the same type of pipe or you’re paying for multiple leak callouts, the “cheap fix” approach can become expensive quickly—especially once repairs trigger venting or shutoff upgrades to meet current code expectations.

Here’s a practical way to think about it. If you have one accessible galvanized leak and only that section needs replacing, you might be able to keep the cost closer to $350–$1,800 for a repair scenario. On the other hand, if you’ve had recurring leaks (2+ in 3 years) and you’re already spending emergency-call dollars and opening walls, a partial repipe can be justified because it reduces repeat labor and future emergency exposure. For full repipes in older homes—especially when galvanized supply lines run through interior walls—many projects move into the $2,000–$7,000 range depending on layout and how much piping is replaced.

What causes low water pressure in Georges homes?
