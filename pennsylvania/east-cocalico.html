What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($883-$2649) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most East Cocalico families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our East Cocalico plumbers can advise based on your usage.
How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?
In East Cocalico, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Hard water in many PA areas accelerates scale buildup and reduces lifespan — annual flushing and an anode rod check extends tank life by 2-4 years. When your heater is over 10 years old and shows signs of rust, inconsistent heating, or visible corrosion, replacement at $883-$2649 is usually smarter than repair.

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by—or under the direct supervision of—a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued by the PA Department of Labor & Industry; you can verify details at dli.pa.gov (and additional information is available through the PA Plumbing Board). For permits, most homeowners should assume the job needs a permit unless the work is clearly a minor repair.

Plumbing permits are commonly required for: new installation of any fixture, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and work that requires inspection by the local code officer. Typical “no permit” items often include fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or doing minor trap or supply adjustments—however, the moment you’re replacing supply piping runs, moving fixtures, or touching sewer connections, you should expect permitting.

Lead service lines: Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from the street to the house) may still be lead in pre-1986 East Cocalico homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the material before work begins.

Workers’ compensation: verify coverage via wcais.pa.gov and request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. This matters because homeowners can be exposed to liability if uninsured workers are injured on the job site.

Step-by-step verification for East Cocalico: (1) confirm the plumber’s license number on the PA DLI/Plumbing Board resources; (2) ask for a current certificate of insurance naming your address as the job location; (3) check workers’ compensation coverage through WC/CAIS; (4) request the permit number (when applicable) so you can confirm inspection status with the municipality.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for East Cocalico homeowners

With 47.9% of East Cocalico’s housing stock dating to pre-1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many Lancaster County homes still have older galvanized steel supply lines and, in some cases, cast-iron drain lines. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out: once the internal scale builds up, flow can drop and pinhole leaks can start appearing more frequently. In contrast, newer copper or PEX piping tends to have fewer recurring leaks, so “fixing the leak” can be penny-wise if the rest of the system is already failing.

The repair-versus-repipe decision should be based on system age, how many leaks you’ve had, water pressure symptoms, and how accessible the piping is. If only one section is accessible and failing, spot repair is often the most economical path. But in East Cocalico, the most cost-effective approach is frequently “partial repipe” or targeted replacement—especially when galvanized runs are concentrated in the same crawlspace zone or near exterior walls where freeze-thaw stresses joints. Winter burst events can also increase the need for proactive work, since a pipe that has already failed once is often closer to failure again.

To ground the decision in dollars: suppose you’re choosing between a single fix and broader replacement. If spot repair would be closer to $250–$1,200 but you already have recurring leaks and water pressure that’s getting worse, a partial repipe may be justified near the $1,100–$800 band for higher-scope work—because each future leak and shutoff-access restoration attempt can quickly exceed the difference. On the other hand, replacing everything when you have one isolated leak in an accessible run is often not the best value.

What causes low water pressure in East Cocalico homes?

Low water pressure in East Cocalico is commonly tied to older plumbing systems and the way older materials age. In many Lancaster County homes built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), galvanized supply lines are a frequent culprit: corrosion buildup narrows the inside diameter over decades, so pressure drops throughout the home. You can also see localized low pressure when sediment or scale clogs aerators, shower valves, or pressure regulators—those are easier wins if the issue is only at one fixture.

Another cause is hidden leaks that don’t always show obvious water pooling. A slow leak on a supply line can reduce effective pressure. In addition, if you’ve had winter freeze events, stressed joints may create micro-leaks that worsen over time. Because East Cocalico sees freeze-thaw cycles, we recommend diagnosis before guessing: plumbers typically check shutoffs, pressure regulator function, and run pressure tests to locate whether the restriction is at the meter, along a branch, or fixture-specific.

Cost-wise, once galvanized corrosion is confirmed, the “diagnose + repipe if galvanized” scope commonly aligns with mid-range replacement budgeting rather than a small repair—often in the vicinity of $1,100–$800 depending on how much pipe is accessible and how much restoration is required.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply pipes coated with zinc. They were widely used decades ago, and in Lancaster County’s older housing stock—where many homes were built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—galvanized supply lines are still common. Over time, the coating and steel corrode from the inside out, which reduces water flow and pressure and eventually causes leaks.

Should you replace them? It depends on the pattern of failure. If you have a single leak in an accessible section, you may be able to spot-repair and defer full replacement. But if you see recurring leaks (two or more in a few years), whole branches showing pressure issues, or corrosion at multiple locations, replacement becomes more cost-effective than repeated repairs and repeat access/patching. Freeze-thaw cycles can also make older joints fail sooner, particularly if pipe runs are in exterior walls or uninsulated crawlspaces.

Pricing for repipe varies drastically based on accessibility and how much piping needs replacement. For budgeting, full repipes in older homes often fall into higher bands—commonly reaching $175–$12,000 when a large portion of the system is replaced. If you’re unsure, ask for a diagnosis with pressure testing and a focused plan (spot vs partial repipe vs full repipe) rather than a one-size-fits-all recommendation.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in East Cocalico winters?

Preventing frozen pipes in East Cocalico comes down to keeping water moving and keeping vulnerable pipe runs warm. Because the area experiences winter temperature swings and freeze-thaw cycles, the biggest risk is plumbing in uninsulated or exterior-wall locations, especially in older homes with older crawlspaces or piping runs. Start by locating shutoffs and insulating exposed supply lines with pipe insulation rated for low temperatures (and insulating tape where appropriate). Pay extra attention to pipes near exterior walls, foundation corners, and areas with drafts.

During deep cold snaps, open cabinet doors on vanities and kitchen sinks to let warmer air circulate around plumbing. If you’ll be away, don’t just “turn everything off”—keep the heat steady, and consider a trickle for the most at-risk fixture line. Also make sure gutters and downspouts are directing water away from foundations so you don’t add moisture and drafts that can stress piping.

If a pipe starts to freeze, avoid sudden high heat. Instead, warm the area gradually using a safe space heater or heating method recommended for plumbing, and keep the water flow minimal but steady once thawing begins. If you end up with a burst, emergency burst-pipe repairs often begin around $250–$1,200 for standard call bands, but winter restoration and access can move costs higher—so prevention is the most budget-friendly approach.
