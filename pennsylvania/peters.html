What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($1027-$3083) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most Peters families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our Peters plumbers can advise based on your usage.
How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?
In Peters, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Hard water in many PA areas accelerates scale buildup and reduces lifespan — annual flushing and an anode rod check extends tank life by 2-4 years. When your heater is over 10 years old and shows signs of rust, inconsistent heating, or visible corrosion, replacement at $1027-$3083 is usually smarter than repair.

If a pipe bursts in your Peters home: 1) Shut off the main water supply immediately (usually near your water meter or where the main enters your home); 2) Turn off the water heater to prevent damage from an empty tank; 3) Open faucets to drain remaining water; 4) Call a licensed Peters emergency plumber — response time matters, water damage compounds fast; 5) Document damage with photos for your homeowner's insurance. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the Pennsylvania Department of Labor & Industry and the PA Plumbing Board—verify status at dli.pa.gov before work begins.

Permits are commonly required for the work types that change system capacity, materials, or require inspection by the local code officer. In Peters, that typically includes:

  • Water heater installation or replacement (including many “same-type” swaps)
  • New fixture installation and any fixture that requires added supply/drain connections
  • Pipe replacement beyond minor repairs (especially when opening walls or changing pipe routing)
  • Sewer line work (repairs/relines, not just cleaning)
  • Any job that will be inspected as part of local code requirements

Repairs that often do not require a permit include fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a flapper, or other like-for-like minor parts. Because municipalities within Washington County can handle inspections and fees differently, confirm requirements with the contractor and the local code office before work opens up walls.

Also, if you’re in Peters with older service piping, ask about lead testing. Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 requires utilities to address lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from street to house) may still contain lead material in pre-1986 homes. Finally, verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov) and require proof of insurance—request a certificate of insurance that lists your property address to reduce homeowner liability if an injury occurs on-site.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel supply lines coated with zinc. In many Pennsylvania homes—especially those built decades ago—galvanized piping is common. Over time, the coating wears away and the interior wall corrodes, causing scale buildup that can lead to leaks, reduced water pressure, and inconsistent hot-water performance. With Peters having a sizable older housing footprint (48.2% pre-1980 per U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), it’s a frequent topic in service calls.

You don’t always need an immediate full repipe the moment you learn the pipe material is galvanized. Some homeowners start with targeted repairs if the failure is isolated and accessible. But when leaks recur, pressure drops throughout the home, or multiple fittings are failing, replacement becomes the safer and more cost-effective route.

To decide, a plumber should identify pipe runs, check for active leaks, test pressure, and recommend partial vs full replacement based on what’s corroded and what’s still sound.
