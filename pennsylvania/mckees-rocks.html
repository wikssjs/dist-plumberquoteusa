What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($895-$2685) but heat standby water 24/7. For most McKees Rocks families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by (or under the direct supervision of) a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the Pennsylvania Department of Labor & Industry (verify at dli.pa.gov) and overseen by the PA Plumbing Board—so before you sign anything, confirm the license type matches the work being done.

Permits are typically required for projects that involve new fixture installation, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, and sewer line work that will be inspected by the local code officer. As a rule of thumb for McKees Rocks homeowners: small “in-kind” repairs like a leaking faucet cartridge, a toilet flapper, or replacing a supply line are usually permit-free, while anything that changes plumbing layout, replaces sections of piping, or requires inspection typically isn’t.

Lead service lines are another compliance topic. Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from street to house) may still be lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so you’re planning correctly and protecting your household.

Finally, verify workers’ compensation coverage through the WCAIS portal (wcais.pa.gov). Request a certificate of insurance listing your property address—homeowners can face liability if uninsured workers get hurt on-site.

Step-by-step: (1) Confirm PA license number via the state registry, (2) ask for a current certificate of insurance naming your address, (3) verify workers’ compensation status in WCAIS, and (4) ensure the quote states who will pull any permit(s) and schedule the inspection.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for McKees Rocks homeowners

With McKees Rocks having 88.4% of housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many Allegheny County homes are running on supply lines that are nearing—or already at—end of service life. Galvanized steel supply lines corrode internally, which can slowly reduce pressure before a more dramatic leak occurs. Cast iron drains and sewer sections can develop cracks and failures at joints over decades, especially with freeze–thaw stress and shifting foundations.

The repair-versus-repipe decision comes down to system condition and how predictable the failure pattern is. If a leak is in a clearly accessible section and the rest of the line shows no signs of deterioration, a spot repair is often the most cost-effective choice. If you’re dealing with recurring leaks (especially multiple incidents across different locations) or you’re seeing discolored water or persistent low pressure, a partial or full repipe can prevent repeat excavation and wall openings.

Here’s the practical way to frame it for McKees Rocks: targeted repairs might fit a $400–$1,500 emergency or standard repair band when the work is limited and access is straightforward. But if the pipe material is galvanized and the failure is systemic, the money you “save” with repeated patching can quickly disappear—especially in finished basements or areas where excavation and restoration drive the labor cost. On sewer work, a spot repair may be far less than a full replacement, but cast iron failures and root intrusion can turn a “minor” job into a bigger scope once camera inspection confirms multiple weak areas.

If you want one clear dollar example: if you’ve already paid for two leaks in three years at roughly $1,500 each, you may be within range of a partial repipe decision sooner than expected—often avoiding additional $1,500–$4,500 emergency bursts later during cold weather.

How to choose a plumbing contractor in McKees Rocks

Choosing the right plumber in McKees Rocks is mostly about verification and paperwork. Start by confirming licensing: in Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be done by or supervised by a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber—check via the state registry (PA DLI) and make sure the license type matches the scope. If the plumber advertises industry certifications (often used for sewer cleaning and inspection work), verify those credentials too—ask for IICRC documentation and confirm the name on the certificate matches the contractor.

Next, request 2–3 itemized written quotes. The best estimates break out labor, materials, and diagnostics (for example: camera inspection line item, jetting/auger option, and disposal/restoration). For warranty, confirm workmanship warranty length and whether equipment/materials carry manufacturer coverage.

For payment schedule, never agree to more than 10–15% upfront. Hold back the remainder until the work is complete and tested. Finally, get an arrival estimate and a completion window in writing—emergency dispatch is different, but planned work in the Pittsburgh market is usually booked days to weeks ahead.

What causes low water pressure in McKees Rocks homes?

A plumber should diagnose methodically: check pressure at the correct test location, isolate fixtures, inspect the water heater inlet/outlet, and evaluate pipe material. If the root cause is galvanized corrosion, the fix may be diagnostic + partial or full repipe—often budgeted above a simple call-out, because addressing the cause prevents repeat low-pressure complaints.
