Plumbing FAQ — Exeter, Pennsylvania

What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($1048-$3144) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most Exeter families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our Exeter plumbers can advise based on your usage.
How do I know if I need to repipe my Exeter home?
Signs you may need repiping in Exeter: low water pressure throughout the home (not just one fixture), discolored or rusty water, frequent leaks in different locations, visible corrosion on pipes, and homes with original galvanized steel pipes (common in PA homes built before 1970). Full-home repiping in copper or PEX runs $733-$2515 depending on home size. A licensed Exeter plumber can inspect and advise — often a camera inspection will confirm if repiping is needed.
How long does it take to replace a water heater in Exeter?
A standard water heater replacement in Exeter by a licensed plumber typically takes 2-4 hours, including draining and removing the old unit, installing the new heater, connecting supply lines, relighting pilots (gas), and testing. Tankless installations may take slightly longer if gas line or electrical upgrades are needed. Most licensed Exeter plumbers offer same-day or next-day water heater replacement — lack of hot water is an urgent issue. Call for a free estimate and confirm availability before booking.
How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?
In Exeter, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Hard water in many PA areas accelerates scale buildup and reduces lifespan — annual flushing and an anode rod check extends tank life by 2-4 years. When your heater is over 10 years old and shows signs of rust, inconsistent heating, or visible corrosion, replacement at $1048-$3144 is usually smarter than repair.
How much does water heater replacement cost in Exeter, PA?
Water heater replacement in Exeter typically costs $1048 to $3144, including the new unit, labor, permit (where required) and disposal of the old heater. A 40-50 gallon gas tank heater (the most common) runs on the lower end. Tankless (on-demand) water heaters cost more upfront but use 24-34% less energy. Electric water heaters are at the lower end of the range. Get a free written estimate from a licensed Exeter plumber — prices vary based on unit type and gas/electric access.
How much does drain cleaning cost in Exeter, PA?
Standard drain cleaning in Exeter — snaking a clogged kitchen or bathroom drain — typically costs $125 to $503. Main sewer line cleaning runs $503 to $1048 depending on access and severity. Hydro-jetting (high-pressure water cleaning for severe buildup or grease) costs more. A video camera inspection to locate blockages or diagnose pipe damage runs $125-$262. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by—or under the direct supervision of—a licensed Master Plumber or a licensed Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry; you can verify credentials at dli.pa.gov and through the PA Plumbing Board resources. Permits also matter: plumbing permits are typically required for new installation of fixtures, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and any work that requires inspection by your local code officer. Simple “like-for-like” repairs—such as replacing a toilet flapper, fixing a leaking faucet, or swapping a stop valve washer—often do not require a permit.

Lead service lines are a common Exeter concern because older neighborhoods may have lead on the homeowner-side lateral (the line from street to house). Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 focuses on utility responsibility for lead service lines; however, the internal homeowner portion can still be lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material before you plan replacement.

Before the job starts, confirm workers’ compensation coverage. Verify coverage via WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov) and ask for a certificate of insurance naming your property address. This protects you from liability if an uninsured worker is injured on your jobsite.

Step-by-step for Exeter homeowners: (1) verify the plumber’s license online (master/journeyman status), (2) request a current certificate of insurance with your address, (3) check workers’ compensation coverage through WCAIS, and (4) confirm which permits will be pulled for your exact scope (water heater, sewer repairs, and pipe replacements usually require them).

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Exeter homeowners

With 43.8% of Exeter’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, many Berks County homes still have older galvanized supply lines or cast-iron drain piping that’s nearing the end of its useful life. Galvanized steel can corrode from the inside out, which gradually reduces water pressure and eventually causes leaks. Cast iron drains tend to last decades, but when they crack or roots intrude, you may get slow drains, recurring backups, and costly, repeated spot repairs. The right decision between targeted repair and partial or full repiping depends on system age, leak frequency, water quality/scale conditions, and how accessible the piping is behind walls, under floors, or in crawlspaces.

If you’re seeing an isolated leak in an accessible section, spot repair often makes sense. But if you have multiple leaks—especially on galvanized piping—or the same section keeps failing, partial repipe becomes more cost-effective than repeating patchwork. For full repipe decisions, consider whether the majority of your supply lines are galvanized and whether removing finishes is already on the table. In Exeter, tight plaster walls and finished basements can mean restoration costs are a major part of the total.

Example: if a single galvanized leak is minor and localized, a spot repair might fall within the lower range of standard repair pricing. But if you’re dealing with recurring leaks across two or three areas, it can be cheaper to repipe more broadly—because replacing one corroded segment often exposes adjacent failures. And if you’re planning other upgrades (bath remodels or a water heater swap), coordinating repiping can reduce the number of times contractors open the same spaces.

Frequently asked questions — plumbing in Exeter

What causes low water pressure in Exeter homes?

Low water pressure in Exeter is usually caused by one of a few common problems, and the cause often ties back to older plumbing materials and freeze–thaw wear. If your home has galvanized supply lines (common in pre-1980 Exeter housing), corrosion can build up inside the pipe over time, shrinking the effective diameter and reducing flow throughout the house. Another frequent driver is partially closed shutoffs, a failing pressure regulator, or sediment issues at fixtures. In some cases, pressure problems show up after a nearby water disruption or after the home’s water heater changes—so it’s important to test pressure at multiple fixtures before replacing parts.

If it’s only one faucet, the issue is often at the fixture, a clogged supply line, or a failing shutoff valve. If it’s whole-house low pressure, it’s more likely a supply-line restriction or a systemic restriction in older piping. A proper diagnosis matters—because correcting a localized valve issue is much cheaper than repiping. If you ultimately need a broader approach due to galvanized corrosion, costs can move into the repipe/major repair ranges rather than a small targeted fix.

Do I need a permit for plumbing work in Exeter?

In Pennsylvania, many plumbing projects require permits and inspections, and Exeter follows the same statewide framework. Plumbing work must be done by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber, and permits are commonly required for new fixture installation, water heater installation, sewer line work, and pipe replacement beyond minor repairs. If the job requires a local code officer inspection, a permit is typically part of the process.

Repairs like replacing a toilet flapper, fixing a leaking faucet, or swapping a supply line or a simple shutoff washer are often handled without a permit because they’re minor and don’t typically change the system’s design or trigger inspection requirements. However, your exact scope matters—especially if you’re opening walls, replacing sections of supply piping, or addressing anything connected to sewer service.

To stay safe in Exeter, ask your plumber to confirm permit requirements before work starts. If you’re choosing between options, permit scope can be one reason one quote comes in lower or higher.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply lines coated to resist corrosion when they were installed. In Exeter homes built before 1980 (which account for about 43.8% of the local housing stock), galvanized piping is common and often shows age-related problems like pinhole leaks, reduced water pressure, and sporadic failures as corrosion progresses internally. Unlike a surface-only issue, corrosion inside the pipe can slowly restrict flow until a leak or pressure drop forces action.

Should you replace them? It depends on condition and your goals. If you have a single accessible leak and the rest of the system looks stable, spot repair may be reasonable. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (for example, multiple leaks over a short period), the more cost-effective choice can be partial or full repiping to copper or PEX—especially because patch jobs in older walls and floors can become expensive once the same area is opened repeatedly.

To make the decision confidently, ask your plumber to evaluate where leaks occur and whether the rest of the system shows signs of generalized corrosion. The “right” move is usually the one that prevents you from paying for repeated emergency repairs.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Exeter winters?

Frozen pipes in Exeter are often driven by exposure: exterior walls, uninsulated crawlspaces, garages, and poorly insulated interior runs can all freeze during cold snaps and rapid temperature swings. Berks County’s freeze–thaw cycles mean the risk isn’t just about deep cold—it’s also about thawing and refreezing, which stresses joints and pipe sections. Prevention focuses on reducing temperature swings and improving insulation.

Practical steps include insulating vulnerable supply lines (especially near hose bibbs), sealing gaps where pipes enter walls, and keeping cabinet doors open on interior plumbing to allow warm air to circulate. If you have an uninsulated crawlspace, prioritize insulation there and consider a freeze-protection approach recommended by a licensed plumber. During severe cold, allow faucets to drip slightly to reduce pressure strain in a vulnerable line. Also, make sure exterior shutoffs and valves actually work before an emergency.

Taking these steps can prevent a burst-pipe call that can escalate quickly—emergency burst repairs can fall in the $350–$3,500 range for typical standard emergency scenarios, and higher when damage is extensive or access is difficult.
