Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by—or under the direct supervision of—a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued by the PA Department of Labor & Industry; you can verify credentials at dli.pa.gov and through the PA Plumbing Board resources.

Permits are commonly required for work such as: installing new plumbing fixtures, replacing plumbing pipe beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, and any sewer line work that must be inspected by the local code official. Many small repairs typically do not require permits, such as fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or other minor component swaps where no new piping is introduced and no inspection is necessary. However, if you’re unsure whether your specific repair crosses the line (for example, cutting into a wall to replace piping), ask your plumber what permit process applies before work begins.

Lead service lines: Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines. Still, the homeowner-side portion of the line (from street to house) can be lead-bearing in older Conewago homes. Ask your plumber to test and document service line material.

Also confirm workers’ compensation coverage using WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov, and request a certificate of insurance showing your property address. To verify a contractor in Conewago, I recommend: (1) check their PA license number online, (2) request a current certificate of insurance, (3) verify workers’ comp coverage through WCAIS, and (4) have the scope list what will be permitted and inspected.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Conewago homeowners

As a practical example, a single isolated repair might land in the $350–$2500 band. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks tied to galvanized supply sections, a partial repipe may be justified even when it’s higher upfront—because every new repair risks disturbing adjacent corroded fittings and can still leave other compromised runs in place.

How to choose a plumbing contractor in Conewago

Choosing a plumber in Conewago is mostly about verifying credentials and matching the scope to the problem. Start by confirming the contractor’s PA license (Master or Journeyman). You can check licensing status through PA Department of Labor & Industry resources at dli.pa.gov. If the job involves sensitive restoration or water damage response, also ask whether they hold any relevant IICRC certification and how it will apply to your situation. For plumbing-related certification documentation, request proof directly and verify it through the provider’s registry where available.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes. Ask for labor and materials breakdowns, not just a lump sum. Look for what’s included and what’s excluded: camera inspection scope, disposal/hauling, permit pulling, drywall/floor restoration coverage, and whether they will return for the “permanent fix” after an emergency stabilization. Warranty matters—ask about workmanship warranty length and separate equipment/material warranties for items like water heaters, shutoff valves, and piping.

For payment, don’t fund the entire project up front. A common homeowner-safe approach is keeping upfront payment around 10–15%, then holding a portion until completion and cleanup. Finally, require a written arrival estimate and a completion window, especially for sewer and water heater work where inspections and parts availability can affect scheduling in York County.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply lines coated with zinc. In Pennsylvania—especially in older homes like many in Conewago built before 1980—galvanized piping can corrode from the inside out. Over time that internal corrosion reduces water pressure, weakens sections, and increases the chance of leaks, which can make repeated repairs feel like whack-a-mole.

Whether you should replace them depends on your pattern. If you have one small, accessible leak and the surrounding pipe is still solid, spot repair may be appropriate. If you have recurring leaks (for example, multiple leaks over a few years), or you see low pressure throughout the house, a partial or full repipe is often the smarter long-term approach. For planning purposes, some homeowners start with smaller repairs in the $350–$2500 band, but repeated failures commonly push the project toward repiping solutions.

Because York County’s freeze–thaw cycles can stress older runs (especially through exterior walls or unconditioned crawlspaces), replacement decisions also hinge on where the pipe travels. A licensed plumber should assess material condition, location, and your repair history before recommending repipe scope.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Conewago winters?

Preventing frozen pipes in Conewago comes down to insulation, reducing drafts, and keeping water moving when the cold snap hits. York County winters can swing quickly, and in older homes with pre-1980 plumbing routes, a burst can happen fast when exterior-wall runs or unconditioned crawlspaces are involved. Start by checking insulation around supply lines in basements, crawlspaces, and anywhere pipes run near exterior walls.

Next, seal air leaks around rim joists, foundation vents, and utility penetrations—cold air movement is what turns a “safe” temperature into freezing conditions. Let faucets drip slightly for vulnerable lines during severe cold, and keep cabinet doors open on plumbing walls so warm indoor air can circulate. If you don’t know where your vulnerable runs are, ask a plumber to do a “winter readiness” check during milder weather.
