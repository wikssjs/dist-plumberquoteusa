What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($998-$2996) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most Upper Allen families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify a contractor at dli.pa.gov (and cross-check any license numbers they provide). For quality and accountability, require proof of licensing before work starts.

Permits are typically required for: (1) new installation of fixtures, (2) water heater installation and replacement, (3) sewer line work (including inspection tie-ins when repair is planned), and (4) pipe replacement beyond minor repairs—especially when local inspection is required by the municipality’s code office under Pennsylvania’s Uniform Construction Code. Many quick fixes—like replacing a toilet flapper, fixing a leaking faucet, or swapping a supply line—often do not require a permit, but it’s still worth asking your plumber what triggers inspection in your specific case.

On lead service lines: while PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, the homeowner-side lateral from the street to the house can still be lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to help you confirm the service line material and document what’s present.

Before signing in Upper Allen, request a certificate of insurance that lists your property address and verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov). A solid process is: verify the license online, match the license number on the invoice, confirm insured status and workers’ comp, then keep those documents in your records for warranty and any future claim support.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Upper Allen homeowners

Upper Allen has a meaningful share of older housing stock—about 34% of homes built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024). In practice, that means many homes still see galvanized steel supply lines and mixed piping sections where corrosion, scale buildup, or failing joints create recurring leaks. Galvanized pipe can corrode from the inside out, leading to reduced pressure and eventual failures. Cast iron drain lines can also reach the point where cracks, joint separation, and root intrusion show up—especially after decades of service.

The repair vs. repipe call depends on system age, the number and pattern of leaks, water pressure symptoms, how accessible the piping is, and whether you’re seeing material deterioration across multiple rooms. If you have one isolated leak in an accessible line—common near mechanical areas—spot repair is usually the most cost-effective path. But if leaks are recurring (for example, multiple failures within a few years) and you’re dealing with galvanized plumbing, partial repipe often becomes justified because the remaining sections are likely to fail soon.

Dollar reality matters. If you’re facing a single accessible leak, a repair in the $350–$1,800 band can be the right move. If the same galvanized run is failing repeatedly, partial repipe guidance often shifts you into the $1,400–$900 style planning range shown for higher-scope work. And in pre-1960 homes with corroded galvanized interior walls or widespread deterioration, full repipe may be the only long-term solution.

What causes low water pressure in Upper Allen homes?

Low water pressure in Upper Allen is usually caused by either supply restrictions upstream of your plumbing or restrictions within your internal piping. In older homes—especially those with galvanized supply lines—corrosion buildup narrows the pipe diameter over time. The result is often “works fine sometimes” at low demand, but noticeable pressure loss when multiple fixtures run or during peak usage.

Another common cause is partial blockage in a supply line due to scale or debris, sometimes after repairs. Less often, pressure regulators can drift out of spec, especially if they’re older. If low pressure is limited to one bathroom or one fixture, the issue might be localized to a shutoff valve, faucet aerator, or a branch line. If the low pressure is throughout the home, it’s more likely a whole-house restriction such as galvanized piping corrosion, a failing supply component, or sediment in the water heater piping.

Because freeze-thaw cycles can stress exposed or poorly insulated sections (and leaks can be hidden until they’re severe), an “it just started” low-pressure complaint deserves a real diagnostic. A licensed plumber can measure pressure at appropriate test points, check the heater connections, and determine whether repipe is warranted versus cleaning or replacing a specific component. If your system is predominantly galvanized, and symptoms are consistent, repipe may be more cost-effective than repeated repairs.
