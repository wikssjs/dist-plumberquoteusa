Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by, or under the direct supervision of, a licensed plumber (Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber). Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify status at https://dli.pa.gov and through the PA Plumbing Board resources. If you’re hiring in South Hanover, this isn’t optional paperwork; it’s tied to how the work is inspected and how responsibility is carried on the job.

Permits are typically required for plumbing work such as: installing new fixtures (including moving or adding supply and drain lines), replacing pipes beyond minor repairs, water heater installation/replacement, sewer line work (repairs and relining), and projects that require local code-officer inspection. By contrast, smaller “swap” repairs—like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or stopping an obvious, accessible supply leak—often do not require a permit.

Be aware of water service line materials. In Pennsylvania, Act 2021-41 requires utilities to replace lead service lines; however, the homeowner-side lateral from the street to the house may still be lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so you know what portion falls under utility replacement.

Also verify liability and coverage. Confirm workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS at https://wcais.pa.gov. Request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. This matters because uninsured work injuries can create serious liability exposure for property owners.

Step-by-step for South Hanover homeowners: (1) ask for the plumber’s license number and verify it online; (2) request a permit plan—who will pull it, and when; (3) confirm workers’ compensation via WCAIS; (4) ask for a current certificate of insurance naming your address; and (5) document all of it before work begins.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for South Hanover homeowners

With 35.5% of South Hanover’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, you’ll often see galvanized steel supply lines and older drain piping approaching end-of-life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, gradually reducing flow and increasing leak frequency. In Dauphin County winters, freeze–thaw cycles can worsen the situation because weakened joints and shallow piping can expand and contract repeatedly, accelerating failure.

Whether you repair or repipe depends on how widespread the problem is and how accessible the piping is. If leaks are isolated and the pipe section is reachable, spot repair is usually the most cost-effective route. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (especially on multiple fixtures), low pressure across the house, or repeated corrosion at fittings, a partial or full repipe often reduces your total downtime and recurring labor. Material choice also matters: modern copper or PEX typically provides more predictable longevity and faster installation than patched galvanized networks.

Here’s a realistic example for a South Hanover homeowner: replacing a single accessible supply leak might stay closer to the lower end of typical burst/leak repair planning. However, if you find a pattern of leaks tied to galvanized runs throughout the house, the investment in partial repipe can be justified because each additional repair tends to open up the same compromised sections again. For homeowners dealing with cast iron drain lines, repair decisions hinge on inspection findings; once cracks or root intrusion appears, targeted work may be paired with sewer scope results, but full relining or more extensive repair becomes more likely.

What causes low water pressure in South Hanover homes?

Low water pressure in South Hanover is often tied to aging supply piping and internal corrosion—particularly in older Dauphin County homes where galvanized lines were common. Over decades, galvanized pipe can develop mineral buildup and restrict flow, leading to weak pressure at multiple fixtures rather than only one. Another frequent cause is a partially blocked water filter (if installed), a failing pressure regulator, or sediment collecting at fittings near the water heater. In some houses, the issue is localized—like a shutoff valve partially closed or a shower cartridge failing—but when low pressure appears throughout the home, corrosion in supply lines is a strong suspect.

Because freeze–thaw cycles can stress aging joints and fittings, pressure problems can become more noticeable after winter temperature swings. If you’re getting whole-house low pressure, it’s reasonable to ask for a diagnosis first; if the piping is galvanized and corroded, repairs may quickly become repetitive and a repipe discussion may be appropriate. Planning costs depend on scope, but if repiping is needed, homeowners often see estimates spanning from the lower end of partial work to full repipe bands (for example, full repipe planning commonly lands within the larger $1,100–$6,500 type ranges depending on layout and material).

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply lines coated with zinc. In many Pennsylvania homes built before 1980, they were installed because they were durable at the time. Over time, galvanized steel corrodes from the inside out, which can reduce water pressure and create frequent leak points at joints and fittings. In South Hanover, where a meaningful portion of housing stock is pre-1980, galvanized lines remain common, and homeowners often experience pressure drop or recurring leaks as they age.

Should you replace them? It depends on condition and how widespread the problem is. If you have a single accessible leak and the rest of the system appears stable, spot repair may be reasonable. But if you have recurring leaks (multiple points), whole-house low pressure, or multiple corrosion failures over a short period, replacement is often the more predictable fix. For budgeting, partial or full repipe planning can range widely based on home layout and material choice (for example, whole-house repipe planning commonly falls within the $1,100–$6,500 type bands, while smaller repair-only work is usually much lower). A licensed plumber can inspect, evaluate corrosion severity, and recommend the least disruptive option.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in South Hanover winters?

Preventing frozen pipes in South Hanover comes down to keeping water moving, insulating vulnerable sections, and reducing temperature swings near supply lines—especially because freeze–thaw cycles can be harsh on older, sometimes shallow, plumbing in Pennsylvania. Start by locating where pipes are most exposed: exterior walls, unheated basements, garages, crawl spaces, and around meter areas. Add insulation sleeves to those sections and, where practical, seal drafts around pipe penetrations. If you have older galvanized or mixed materials, pay extra attention to joints and fittings because corrosion and age can make failures more likely when temperatures swing quickly.

During extreme cold, allow a small trickle of water from a frequently used faucet to relieve pressure and keep flow going. Keep cabinet doors open in winter if plumbing runs through kitchens or bathrooms on exterior walls. If you’re leaving town, don’t shut off heat; maintain a consistent indoor temperature. For emergency readiness, know that burst-pipe responses are commonly in a higher band (for planning, burst-pipe repair often lands within the $350–$2,500 standard range, and can be higher for true emergencies). The best “savings” is preventing the freeze event that forces emergency excavation and restoration.
