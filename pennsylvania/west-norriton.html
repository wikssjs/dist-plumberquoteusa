Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by—or under the direct supervision of—a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are regulated through the PA Department of Labor & Industry (verify at dli.pa.gov) and the PA Plumbing Board. For West Norriton homeowners, the practical takeaway is that you should not assume “someone with a truck” is licensed for permit-required work.

Plumbing permits are typically required for: new fixture installations, any pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation/replacement, sewer line work, and work that requires local code officer inspection. Examples that commonly do not require a permit include fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or making like-for-like repairs that do not involve new pipe or structural changes.

On the water service side, Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 addresses lead service lines for utilities, but in many older West Norriton homes built before 1986, the homeowner-side lateral (from the street to the house) can still be lead. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so you know what part is truly being replaced and what health risks remain.

Before work begins, verify three items: (1) licensing credentials (use online registry sources), (2) general liability and—crucially—certificate of insurance listing your property address, and (3) workers’ compensation coverage by checking via WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov). Request a certificate of workers’ comp coverage proof; homeowners can face liability if uninsured workers are injured on their property.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for West Norriton homeowners

With about 56% of West Norriton housing stock dating to pre-1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many homes still have galvanized steel supply lines and—on the drainage side—cast iron systems nearing the end of their practical service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, often causing intermittent pressure issues, slow leaks at threaded joints, and eventual pinhole failures. Cast iron drains typically last decades, but once roots or cracking appear, the system becomes harder to keep “patch-stable.”

Repair versus repipe is best decided by pattern and accessibility. A single leak in an accessible section may justify spot repair, especially if the surrounding pipe is still solid and no recurring failures are showing up. But if you’re seeing multiple leaks across bathrooms/kitchens or pressure dropping over time, it’s usually a sign the corrosion is systemic and spot repairs become a cycle. In the Montgomery County climate, freeze–thaw periods also amplify small leaks: a minor seep can become a burst when the line freezes in an uninsulated crawlspace or along exterior runs.

Here’s a practical dollar example: if you’re choosing between spot repair in the $400–$1,800 band and partial repipe closer to $1,200–$950, the cost difference may be justified when you’ve had two or more leaks within a few years—because the “saved” repair quickly disappears after the next failure.

Our approach in West Norriton is to document material condition, identify where the failure is concentrated, and match the repair scope to the age and risk level of the rest of the piping.

What causes low water pressure in West Norriton homes?
