What should I do if a pipe bursts in Yeadon?
If a pipe bursts in your Yeadon home: 1) Shut off the main water supply immediately (usually near your water meter or where the main enters your home); 2) Turn off the water heater to prevent damage from an empty tank; 3) Open faucets to drain remaining water; 4) Call a licensed Yeadon emergency plumber — response time matters, water damage compounds fast; 5) Document damage with photos for your homeowner's insurance. How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?
In Yeadon, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Hard water in many PA areas accelerates scale buildup and reduces lifespan — annual flushing and an anode rod check extends tank life by 2-4 years. What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($878-$2636) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most Yeadon families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our Yeadon plumbers can advise based on your usage.

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber (you can verify licenses through the PA Department of Labor & Industry and the PA Plumbing Board at dli.pa.gov). That’s not just a formality—licensed plumbers are also the ones who can legally pull permits when required.

Permits are typically required for work such as: installing a new fixture, replacing plumbing piping beyond minor repairs, installing a water heater, performing sewer line work, and any job that the local code officer will inspect. In contrast, minor repairs like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or swapping stop valves usually do not require a permit.

Lead service lines are an important Pennsylvania-specific issue. While PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, older Yeadon homes may still have lead in the homeowner-side lateral (from the street to the house). Ask your plumber to test and document service line material before you plan replacement so you know what portion is regulated and what portion may still be lead.

For safety and liability, confirm workers’ compensation coverage using WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov. Also request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. Homeowners can face liability exposure if uninsured workers get hurt on a property.

To verify credentials step-by-step: (1) check the plumber’s license number in the PA registry, (2) confirm they’re permitted/licensed for the specific job scope (heater/sewer/water piping), (3) review their insurance certificate, and (4) verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS—then keep copies with your invoice.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Yeadon homeowners

In Yeadon, the “repair vs. repipe” decision is especially sensitive because about 94.1% of homes were built before 1980. That means many properties still rely on galvanized steel supply lines and/or older cast-iron drain systems approaching the end of their practical service life. Galvanized supply corrodes from the inside out, often first showing up as reduced water pressure, stubborn leaks at threaded joints, and uneven flow to fixtures. Cast iron drains can develop cracks, scale internally, and become susceptible to root intrusion in areas where laterals are older or joints have shifted.

Here’s how homeowners should think about it: if the leak is truly isolated to an accessible section and the rest of the system looks sound, targeted repair is usually the best value. But if you’re seeing repeating leaks (or if diagnostic results point to widespread corrosion), partial or full repiping can be cheaper than repeated emergency repairs. A useful rule of thumb in Yeadon’s older housing: when the cause is corrosion in a system that’s already decades old, you’re often paying the “same problem” repeatedly unless the pipe is replaced.

For a specific dollar example, if you’re dealing with recurring leaks in galvanized supply, a partial repipe may fall around $1,000–$1,400. If instead you keep patching individual leaks, each repair can land in lower single digits to higher costs depending on access and drywall/floor restoration; over a year or two, the total typically exceeds the repipe cost. On cast iron drain issues showing cracks or root intrusion, the decision often starts with a sewer scope, then moves to spot repair or reline—commonly $3,200–$7,000 depending on the extent.

Ultimately, the right recommendation depends on system age, leak frequency, water pressure symptoms, and how much of the piping run is accessible without major demolition.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply lines coated with zinc, common in older PA homes and still seen in many Yeadon properties built before 1960. Over decades, galvanized steel corrodes from the inside out, which can reduce water pressure and eventually cause leaks at joints or failing pipe sections. Whether you should replace them depends on symptoms and frequency of failures. If you have one isolated leak in an accessible section, spot repair may be enough. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (for example, 2+ leaks over a few years) or widespread low pressure, replacement is often the more cost-effective move because repairs keep targeting the same corroded network. How do I prevent frozen pipes in Yeadon winters?

To prevent frozen pipes in Yeadon and across Pennsylvania, focus on keeping pipes warm and reducing drafts in the areas where old homes are most vulnerable—uninsulated wall cavities, crawlspaces, and basement corners. Because 94.1% of homes were built before 1980, insulation and pipe tracing in exterior runs may be limited compared with newer construction.
