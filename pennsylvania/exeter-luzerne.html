Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licensing is issued by the Pennsylvania Department of Labor & Industry; you can verify credentials at dli.pa.gov and through the PA Plumbing Board.

Permits are typically required for: new installation of any fixture, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and any job that must be inspected by the local code officer. In contrast, many small repairs—like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or addressing a simple stop-valve issue—often do not require a permit. Because Exeter’s rules are municipality-specific for inspection timing, a licensed contractor should check with the local code office before scheduling.

Lead service lines are also important. PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but in older Exeter homes (especially pre-1986), the homeowner-side lateral from the street to the house can still be lead. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material before you disturb anything.

Insurance and workers’ compensation matter too. Confirm workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov, and request a certificate of insurance that lists your property address. If a worker is injured on your property without coverage, the risk can fall on the homeowner.

Step-by-step in Exeter: (1) verify the plumber’s license online before the work begins, (2) ask whether a permit is required and who will pull it, (3) request proof of insurance and WC via certificate and WCAIS results, and (4) keep those documents with your contract for your records.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Exeter homeowners

With 69.4% of Exeter’s housing stock built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many Luzerne County homes still have galvanized steel supply lines or cast iron drain lines nearing the end of their service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, which can start as slow degradation (pin-hole leaks, sputtering, or pressure loss) and later turn into repeated breaks. Cast iron drains often last decades, but once you see cracks or root intrusion, the failure pattern typically escalates—particularly during freeze periods when soil movement and moisture expand cracks.

The repair vs. repipe decision depends on system age, how often leaks occur, water quality concerns, and how accessible the piping is for replacement. If only one small section is failing and the rest is accessible and in good condition, spot repair can be the cost-effective approach. But recurring leaks (2+ in a few years), widespread corrosion signs, or multiple discolored/low-pressure areas are strong indicators that partial or full repipe will cost less over time than repeated emergency response.

For a grounded example: a single accessible leak in an open section of basement piping is often workable as a $250–$1,800 spot repair. However, if you have galvanized supply lines and you’re seeing the same problem repeatedly, a partial repipe may be justified—using the cost band of $1,400–$950 as a planning reference (scope-dependent)—because you reduce future break risk and avoid repeated wall or drywall restoration.

Because Exeter has a large share of pre-1980 housing stock (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), galvanized lines are still common, and they can corrode from the inside out long before the damage is obvious. A first step is diagnosis: have a licensed plumber check pipe material, inspect access points, and measure pressure and flow. What causes low water pressure in Exeter homes?

You may notice pressure drops at certain fixtures, slower hot-water recovery, or intermittent pressure issues. Another cause is partial shutoff valves installed incorrectly, sediment buildup in older water heaters, or pressure regulator problems (if present). In homes built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), leaks may also be a factor—sometimes the leak is behind a wall and only shows up as pressure loss rather than a visible drip. The fix depends on the diagnosis: a plumber may test pressure at the source, inspect accessible piping for corrosion, and determine whether you’re seeing a localized obstruction or a whole-line restriction. What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel pipes with a zinc coating that were commonly installed in older homes. In Exeter and across Luzerne County, galvanized supply lines are still found in many pre-1980 properties (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024). Over time, galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, which can reduce water pressure, discolor water, and eventually cause leaks or breaks—sometimes during freeze-thaw cycles when water expands in and around fittings. Whether you should replace them depends on condition and your risk tolerance. If you have one small, accessible leak and other sections look healthy, a spot repair may be reasonable (often near $250–$1,800). But if leaks are recurring, pressure is low throughout, or corrosion signs show up across multiple areas, replacement or partial repiping may cost less than repeated emergency repairs. A licensed plumber can evaluate by checking accessible sections, measuring pressure, and discussing what kind of access you’ll have to upgrade piping. If repiping is planned alongside a remodel, replacement is often more practical because walls and flooring may already be open.
