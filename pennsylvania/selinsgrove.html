How long does a water heater last in Pennsylvania?

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licences are issued by the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify status and credentials at dli.pa.gov or through the PA Plumbing Board resources. For homeowners in Selinsgrove, the practical takeaway is simple: if the contractor can’t show current licensing for the work you’re hiring them to do, you should pause before scheduling.

Permits are required for many categories, including: new installation of any fixture (for example, adding a new bathroom fixture), pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and any work that requires inspection by the local code officer. In contrast, minor repairs like fixing a leaking faucet or replacing a toilet flapper typically do not require a permit because there’s no meaningful new piping or inspection scope change.

Two compliance items homeowners often miss: (1) Lead service lines. Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 requires utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral from the street to the house may still be lead in older Selinsgrove properties. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material. (2) Workers’ compensation. Verify coverage using WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov. Also request a certificate of insurance listing your property address—without proof, homeowners can assume liability if an uninsured worker is injured on-site.

Step-by-step: confirm license using PA’s online resources; request a COI and verify the workers’ comp coverage through WCAIS; then ask your plumber to outline which portions require inspection/permit so you’re not surprised by schedule delays.

Next, request 2–3 itemized written quotes. The best estimates break down labor and materials (for example: cut-and-repair lengths, fittings, disposal/hauling, testing), rather than one lump-sum number. What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are older steel water supply lines coated to resist corrosion. In many pre-1980 Selinsgrove homes, galvanized pipe is common, and it can fail due to internal corrosion that doesn’t always show immediately. Over time, corrosion reduces water pressure and can lead to leaks. Whether you should replace galvanized pipe depends on how your system is behaving—single isolated leaks may be handled with spot repairs, but frequent failures are usually a sign the pipe is nearing end-of-life.

Because Snyder County has a lot of older housing stock—about 87.2% of homes built before 1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024)—it’s not unusual to see galvanized sections that have outlived their practical lifespan. If you have recurring leaks (for example, 2+ in 3 years), many homeowners choose partial or full repiping rather than paying repeated emergency premiums. How do I prevent frozen pipes in Selinsgrove winters?

Preventing frozen pipes in Selinsgrove starts with reducing cold exposure and protecting vulnerable line routes. Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycles are a major driver of winter burst repairs, especially in older homes where lines may be shallow, uninsulated, or routed through basements/crawlspaces. Begin by checking areas that tend to get cold: exterior walls, near drafts, under sinks that back onto unheated spaces, and any basement/crawlspace runs.

Practical steps: seal air leaks around pipe penetrations, insulate exposed piping, and keep cabinet doors open on exterior-wall kitchens/baths. During extreme cold, let faucets drip slightly for lines most at risk—this can relieve pressure in frozen sections. If you’ve had previous winter freeze events, consider improving insulation in the specific areas where problems occurred rather than only focusing on “whole home” measures.

Finally, know what “early warning” looks like: reduced water flow, unusual banging, or partial pressure loss can indicate an ice restriction before a full burst. If you suspect freezing in an older galvanized system, call for diagnosis early; emergency repairs and burst pipe work can move into the $350–$2,200 standard-to-emergency range quickly depending on access and restoration needs.
