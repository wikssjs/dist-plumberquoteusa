What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($865-$2595) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most New Holland families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by—or under the direct supervision of—a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry, and you can verify status at dli.pa.gov and through the PA Plumbing Board resources. For homeowners in New Holland, this isn’t just paperwork: it’s how you know the work is being done to code and inspected when required.

Permits are commonly required for plumbing that involves new installations of fixtures, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and any job that requires inspection by the local code officer. Repairs like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or swapping a supply line connector are typically treated as minor repairs and may not require a permit—but your plumber should confirm based on your exact scope.

Lead service lines are a special concern statewide. PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from the street to the house) may still contain lead in pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so you understand what responsibility falls where.

Also confirm workers’ compensation coverage before work begins. Verify coverage via wcais.pa.gov and request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. If a contractor isn’t properly covered, homeowners can be exposed to liability for injuries.

To verify credentials step-by-step: (1) ask for the plumber’s license number and verify it on the PA registry; (2) obtain a COI naming you/your address and confirming general liability; (3) confirm workers’ compensation through WCAIS and keep the confirmation or written proof in your records; (4) ensure the company states it will pull any required permits before scheduling inspections.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for New Holland homeowners

With roughly 69.5% of housing in the New Holland area built before 1980, many Lancaster County homes still have older galvanized steel supply lines and cast-iron drain piping. Galvanized pipe often corrodes from the inside out, which can cause intermittent leaks, reduced pressure, and eventual failure. Cast iron drains can crack with age and shift, and they can also be susceptible to root intrusion. When these systems start “collecting” issues—slow flow in multiple fixtures, repeated leaks, or stubborn drainage problems—it can be more economical to repipe than to keep chasing the next failure.

The decision depends on how much of the system is accessible and whether the failure pattern is localized. If the leak is in an exposed run and the surrounding pipe is sound, a targeted spot repair is reasonable. But if leaks happen repeatedly in different locations, especially in older galvanized runs, the odds are high that the corrosion is systemic, and a partial repipe is often the cost-effective middle step. In many homes, a full repipe becomes justified when multiple branches fail or when corrosion and reduced pressure suggest the interior diameter has been significantly reduced.

Here’s one practical dollar example: if you’re choosing between repairing one leaking section versus converting the house to copper or PEX, a one-off repair may fall around $250–$1,200, but repeated patchwork can quickly exceed the cost of a staged approach. If a partial repipe becomes necessary, pricing can move into the $1,100–$8,00 style band depending on access and number of runs—while a full repipe can reach much higher if walls or floors must be opened throughout.

Freezing conditions also matter. Lancaster County’s winter swings raise burst risk in exterior-wall piping and uninsulated runs. When that environment has already “proven” the pipe is vulnerable, proactive repipe planning can prevent a future emergency.

When should I repipe my New Holland home?

Most New Holland homeowners start thinking about repiping when they’re no longer dealing with isolated problems. What causes low water pressure in New Holland homes?

Low water pressure in New Holland is usually caused by one of a few issues, and the age of your home often narrows the possibilities. In homes with older galvanized piping (common in pre-1980 housing—about 69.5% in the local profile), corrosion can reduce the internal diameter over decades. That corrosion gradually restricts flow, which may show up as weaker pressure at multiple fixtures. (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024). Another common cause is partial valve issues—like a partially closed shutoff, a water pressure regulator that’s out of adjustment, or a failing mixing valve.

Sometimes the problem is localized: a clogged aerator, a failing fixture cartridge, or a supply-line restriction near one bathroom. But if the low pressure affects most or all fixtures, it points more toward the supply system—especially in older homes where multiple runs may be compromised.

Freeze-thaw cycles can also contribute by causing intermittent flow problems or encouraging micro-leaks that worsen over time. If you recently had a cold-weather event, mention it to your plumber. If diagnosis indicates corrosion throughout the house, the solution may extend beyond minor repairs and can lead into repipe discussions, where costs can vary widely depending on access—frequently requiring a staged plan to control disruption.
