What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($930-$2791) but heat standby water 24/7. For most Connellsville families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. In Connellsville, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Hard water in many PA areas accelerates scale buildup and reduces lifespan — annual flushing and an anode rod check extends tank life by 2-4 years. When your heater is over 10 years old and shows signs of rust, inconsistent heating, or visible corrosion, replacement at $930-$2791 is usually smarter than repair.

Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work generally must be performed by, or under the direct supervision of, a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify using the resources at dli.pa.gov and the PA Plumbing Board. In Connellsville and across Fayette County, this matters because many “repair calls” turn into permit-required scope once the plumber sees the full condition.

Permits are typically required for: installation of new plumbing fixtures, replacement of plumbing pipes beyond minor repair, water heater installation, sewer line work (repair, replacement, inspection tied to repair scope), and any work requiring local code-officer inspection. Simple homeowner-adjacent fixes like replacing a toilet flapper or repairing a leaky faucet handle usually do not require permits.

Lead service lines are another important point. PA Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the service line from the street to your house (the homeowner-side lateral in many older homes) can still be lead in pre-1986 properties. Ask your plumber to test and document what material is present before excavation or line replacement.

Before work begins, confirm workers’ compensation coverage using wcais.pa.gov. Also request a certificate of insurance that lists your property address (or at least names you as an insured interest where applicable) to reduce risk. To verify a contractor, check their PA license online, then match the license name to the invoice/permit. Next, review the certificate of insurance and WC proof; ask for updated documents and keep copies for your records.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Connellsville homeowners

With Connellsville housing that’s predominantly older (89.2% of dwellings built before 1980), many Fayette County homes still have galvanized steel supply lines and, in some cases, aging copper. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, often causing symptoms like reduced hot water volume, inconsistent pressure between fixtures, and leaks that seem to return after “temporary” fixes. Cast-iron drain lines can show cracks, joint leaks, or root intrusion—issues that usually don’t stay small once they begin.

The repair-versus-repipe decision comes down to system age, how often leaks occur, water quality concerns, and how accessible the plumbing is. If a single leak is in an accessible section and there’s no sign the rest of the run is deteriorating, a targeted spot repair can be the most economical path. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (for example, multiple failures within a short period), the odds increase that the corrosion is systemic rather than isolated.

Here’s where the money math typically lands in Connellsville: a spot repair might be reasonable when it’s truly one localized failure (often around the $350–$1,800 range). If that same home has galvanized supply lines with repeated leaks, homeowners sometimes spend less long-term by moving to a partial repipe—still a significant project, but it prevents the cycle of opening walls and re-plumbing the same system. In pre-1960 homes, a full repipe to copper or PEX can be much higher (up to $2,000–$7,000), but it can be justified if corroded interior walls and widespread mineral buildup make future failures likely.

For drains and sewers, the same “scope reality” applies: a small repair may work if the issue is isolated, but damaged cast iron with root intrusion often requires sewer camera inspection and spot repair or reline. For most homeowners, the best first step is an assessment—often with a pressure or flow test and, for drains, a camera inspection—to avoid paying for repairs that don’t address the cause.

What causes low water pressure in Connellsville homes?

Low water pressure in Connellsville is often caused by either a utility-side issue or—more commonly in older Fayette County housing—internal pipe corrosion and buildup. With many homes built before 1980, galvanized pipe is a frequent culprit because corrosion narrows the inside diameter over time and can create inconsistent flow between fixtures. You may notice that some faucets are strong while others are weak, or that hot water pressure is noticeably lower than cold. Freeze–thaw cycles can also contribute indirectly: bursts and repeated repairs may lead to valve or fitting issues, and older systems sometimes have outdated shutoff locations that complicate isolation and repairs. Another common cause is clogged aerators, pressure-reducing valves that are out of adjustment, or sediment buildup at the water heater. If your plumber diagnoses it as a plumbing-system issue, the solution may start with evaluation and flushing, but if galvanized is widespread, a repipe plan may be more cost-effective than repeated repairs. What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water supply lines with a zinc coating that was widely used in older homes. In Connellsville and much of Fayette County, these are common in pre-1980 housing (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), and they can fail due to internal corrosion “from the inside out.” When galvanized pipe deteriorates, homeowners often see reduced pressure, discolored water, recurring leaks, and frequent repairs in the same general areas. Whether you should replace them depends on how much of the system is affected and how the failures present. If it’s one accessible leak in a small section, a spot repair may be reasonable. But if you have recurring leaks, pressure problems throughout the house, or multiple valves/fittings already showing weakness, replacement is often the better long-term solution. In that case, a partial repipe or full repipe to copper or PEX may be recommended, and pricing can vary widely based on access and how many runs need replacement. Because freeze–thaw cycles can stress older pipework in uninsulated areas, it’s smart to address galvanized issues proactively rather than waiting for the next winter burst.
