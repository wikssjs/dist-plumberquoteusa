Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by (or under the direct supervision of) a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry—verify status at dli.pa.gov and, if needed, confirm details through the PA Plumbing Board. For Hanover homeowners, this matters because even when a fix looks minor, underground piping and water heater changes often require inspection.

Plumbing permits are typically required for: new installation of any fixture, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation/replacement, sewer line work, and any work that requires local code-officer inspection. Repairs like fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or swapping a supply line connector generally do not require a permit because they’re considered minor maintenance and usually don’t require an inspection.

Lead service lines are a special case. While the PA Act 2021-41 focuses on utilities replacing lead service lines, the homeowner-side lateral (from the street to the house) may still contain lead in older Hanover homes built before 1986. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material before disturbing piping.

Also confirm insurance and workers’ compensation. Verify workers’ compensation coverage at wcais.pa.gov, and request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. Step-by-step, you can: (1) check the plumber’s license online, (2) confirm they can pull any required permit, (3) review the certificate of insurance, and (4) verify workers’ compensation coverage before work begins.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Hanover homeowners

With roughly 82% of Hanover-area housing built before 1980, it’s common to see galvanized steel supply lines and older drain piping nearing the end of their useful life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out—first slowing flow, then causing leaks as the wall thins. Cast iron drains can also last a long time, but once you see cracks, corrosion, or root intrusion, repairs can become repeated “patch-and-replace.” In York County, the freeze–thaw cycle can worsen these issues, especially when insulation is limited or piping routes through exterior walls and unconditioned spaces.

Whether you should spot-repair, partially repipe, or go full repipe depends on system age, the pattern of failures, and how accessible the piping is. If there’s one leak in an easily accessed run, a spot repair often makes sense. But if you’re dealing with recurring leaks (especially on galvanized runs), partial repiping can be a practical middle step—until the rest of the system starts failing. If the majority of your supply piping is galvanized, or if you’re frequently losing pressure or experiencing multiple failures, full repipe can prevent the cost of chasing problems room by room.

Dollar-wise, the difference can be justified or not. For example, fixing a single accessible leak might fall in the $350–$2,500 range, while recurring leaks on galvanized supply lines often move into partial repipe territory (about $900–$800 in this estimate band, depending on what must be opened and how much is replaced). If you wait too long on galvanized systems, the long-term cost can climb quickly because you end up paying for multiple wall openings, returns, and repairs.

How to choose a plumbing contractor in Hanover

Choosing the right plumber in Hanover starts with credentials and clarity. First, verify licensing: Pennsylvania requires plumbing work by or under a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Check the license directly through the PA Department of Labor & Industry (dli.pa.gov) and confirm the specific license holder who will be responsible onsite. If the job includes remediation-related cleaning or specialized procedures, also ask about IICRC certification when applicable and verify it through the IICRC directory or provider documentation.

Next, get 2–3 itemized written quotes. You want a breakdown of labor and materials—not a single lump sum—so you can compare like-for-like (pipe type, fittings, valves, disposal, permits, and restoration). Read the scope for exclusions: for example, what’s included for drywall access, flooring removal, trench backfill, camera inspection, and whether restoration is part of the price or billed separately. If you’re considering an insurance claim, ask how they document conditions (photos, measurements, and repair notes).

Warranty matters. Ask for a workmanship warranty length and whether equipment and materials carry separate warranties (water heater manufacturer warranty vs. installation warranty). Payment should be conservative—avoid paying more than 10–15% upfront and request a holdback until completion. Finally, insist on a written arrival estimate and a completion window, especially for emergency calls; after-hours work often requires a follow-up for permanent restoration.

Frequently asked questions — plumbing in Hanover

Do I need a permit for plumbing work in Hanover?

In Hanover and across Pennsylvania, permit requirements depend on the type of plumbing work. Generally, Pennsylvania requires plumbing work to be done by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber, and many projects also require local permit/inspection. Permits are typically required for water heater installation/replacement, sewer line work, and pipe replacement beyond minor repairs—especially when the local code officer must inspect. Minor maintenance like replacing a toilet flapper, repairing a faucet, or swapping an internal connector usually does not require a permit. Because permit rules can vary by municipality around York County, a reputable Hanover plumber will ask what you’re planning and tell you whether they will pull a permit and schedule inspection. If you’re replacing a gas water heater or touching venting, expect permitting. If you’re only correcting a small leak at a valve or within an accessible supply line, permits are often not needed.

How much does drain cleaning cost in Hanover?

Drain cleaning costs in Hanover usually depend on whether it’s a simple stoppage or a deeper main-line issue that requires hydro-jetting and/or camera inspection. For typical York County pricing, drain cleaning commonly falls within $350–$900, with the range reflecting how long the blockage has been present and how difficult it is to access. What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are older steel supply lines coated with zinc. They were common in Pennsylvania homes built decades ago, including many Hanover properties. Over time, the inside of galvanized pipe corrodes, which can lead to reduced water flow, pressure issues, discoloration, and eventually leaks. Whether you should replace them depends on what you’re seeing: if you have a single, accessible leak, a spot repair may be enough. But if you’re dealing with recurring leaks or system-wide problems—especially in homes where a large share of plumbing is galvanized—replacement often costs less in the long run than repeatedly paying for access and patching. In Hanover, where much of the housing stock is older (81.7% pre-1980), pipe corrosion can show up sooner due to how lines are routed and insulated. If you’re considering repiping, a plumber should assess pressure, inspect exposed sections, and recommend whether you need spot repair, partial repipe, or full repipe based on the condition of the piping throughout your home.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Hanover winters?

Preventing frozen pipes in Hanover is mostly about reducing temperature swings and keeping water moving where needed. York County’s freeze–thaw cycles can quickly turn a slightly exposed run into a burst risk, especially in older homes where plumbing passes through exterior walls, unconditioned crawlspaces, or near vents. Start by insulating pipes in unheated areas (foam pipe insulation plus sealing around gaps), then ensure warm air reaches key spaces—like allowing cabinet doors to stay open where plumbing is behind them. Fix drafts around windows and where plumbing penetrates exterior walls. If a deep cold snap is forecast, let faucets drip slightly at the coldest end of the house to relieve pressure. For homes with older galvanized lines, insulation alone can help, but you also want to address any missing insulation or damaged vapor barriers along exterior routes. If you’ve already had a freeze issue before, consider adding insulation upgrades and improving access for future maintenance. A plumber can also help confirm which routes are most vulnerable during winter.
