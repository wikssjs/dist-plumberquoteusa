Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued by the PA Department of Labor & Industry, and homeowners can verify credentials at dli.pa.gov or through the PA Plumbing Board resources.

Permits are commonly required for: new installation of any plumbing fixture, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and any work that needs inspection by the local code officer or municipal authority. In Hermitage, like other Mercer County jurisdictions, rules are usually straightforward for like-for-like repairs, but permitting becomes more likely when the job changes the system or requires inspection before it’s covered.

Repairs that typically do not require a permit include fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, unclogging a drain, or minor restoration that doesn’t alter piping or vents. However, if you’re replacing shutoffs, relocating drains, modifying venting, or doing any sewer connection work, ask your plumber what the permit/inspection path will be.

For lead service lines: Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 focuses on utility replacement of lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from the street to the house) in older Hermitage homes may still contain lead, especially in pre-1986 properties. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material.

Workers’ compensation matters, too. Verify coverage via WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov, and request an insurance certificate listing your property address. Step-by-step: confirm the license online, ask for the certificate of insurance, verify workers’ comp on WCAIS, and keep copies with your project paperwork.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Hermitage homeowners

With a large portion of Hermitage housing built before 1980, many Mercer County homes still include galvanized steel supply lines and older drain materials that can be nearing the end of their service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out, gradually reducing water pressure and increasing the likelihood of leaks at joints, valves, and threaded fittings. Cast iron drain lines, while durable, can develop cracks and joint failures over time—especially when root intrusion or settlement has begun.

Choosing spot repair versus partial or full repipe depends on age, how frequently you’re seeing leaks, whether the problem repeats across multiple rooms, and how accessible the piping is. In Hermitage, where older basements and crawlspaces are common, access can be decent for certain sections, but wall runs may be hidden behind drywall or finished trim. That means the “cheapest fix today” can become expensive if the rest of the system is similarly corroded.

For example, if you have one isolated leak in an accessible section of pipe, spot repair may be the right call—often in the $325–$1450 band depending on location and material. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (two or more in a three-year period) on galvanized supply lines, the savings from repeated repairs can disappear quickly, and homeowners often end up paying for multiple cut-and-repair cycles when a partial repipe may make more sense.

Where access is limited and corrosion is widespread, a full repipe can be justified despite its higher ticket. If you’re already dealing with whole-home water pressure problems, the diagnosis is typically more reliable when your plumber reviews water pressure trends and identifies the sections that are most restricted. Always ask for the “why” behind the recommendation: what material is failing, where it’s failing, and what will likely fail next.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Hermitage winters?

Frozen pipes are a real risk in Hermitage during sharp cold snaps because freeze-thaw cycles stress older piping, and many homes built before 1980 still have runs in basements, crawlspaces, exterior wall cavities, garages, and poorly insulated chases. Start by locating shutoffs and making sure they actually turn; seized shutoffs can turn a minor freeze into an emergency burst. Let faucets drip slightly for pipes that run through unheated areas, and keep cabinet doors open where plumbing is inside vanity base cabinets. Seal drafts around where pipes enter the house, and consider adding insulation sleeves to exposed sections.

At night, maintain consistent indoor temperatures (space heaters can help, but be safe and avoid blocking combustion-air requirements). If you’re going away, keep the thermostat at a steady setting rather than turning it off completely. In a winter emergency, burst-pipe repair can land in a high band—often $1450–$3950 depending on mitigation and access—so prevention is usually cheaper than emergency water restoration.

If you’ve had freeze trouble before, ask your plumber about a targeted plan for the specific affected runs (not just “wrap everything”).
