What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($928-$2784) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most O'Hara families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Our O'Hara plumbers can advise based on your usage.
How do I know if I need to repipe my O'Hara home?
Signs you may need repiping in O'Hara: low water pressure throughout the home (not just one fixture), discolored or rusty water, frequent leaks in different locations, visible corrosion on pipes, and homes with original galvanized steel pipes (common in PA homes built before 1970). Full-home repiping in copper or PEX runs $618-$2062 depending on home size. A licensed O'Hara plumber can inspect and advise — often a camera inspection will confirm if repiping is needed.

Most licensed O'Hara plumbers offer same-day or next-day water heater replacement — lack of hot water is an urgent issue. Call for a free estimate and confirm availability before booking.

Water heater replacement in O'Hara typically costs $928 to $2784, including the new unit, labor, permit (where required) and disposal of the old heater. In O'Hara, a tank water heater typically lasts 8-12 years (gas) or 10-15 years (electric). Tankless water heaters last 15-20+ years. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by (or under the direct supervision of) a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. License information is issued through the PA Department of Labor & Industry (verify at dli.pa.gov) and overseen by the Pennsylvania Plumbing Board—always confirm credentials before work begins. For homeowners in O’Hara, this isn’t just best practice; it protects you if the city or local code officer requires corrections after an inspection.

Permits are typically required for: installation of new fixtures, any pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation, sewer line work, and work that needs local inspection. Repairs like replacing a toilet flapper or fixing a leaking faucet are usually minor and commonly do not require a plumbing permit. However, if plumbing involves replacing supply lines, touching a building sewer, or changing venting, you should assume a permit may apply.

Lead service lines: Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 drives replacement of lead service lines by the utility, but the homeowner-side lateral (from street to house) can still be lead in older O’Hara homes built before the 1986 rule change. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material and coordinate with the utility if lead is present.

Workers’ compensation: verify coverage via WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov). Also request a certificate of insurance showing your property address and coverage details, and confirm workers’ comp is active for the company performing the work.

To verify step-by-step, (1) confirm the plumber’s PA license at the DLI/Plumbing Board resources, (2) ask for the company’s certificate of insurance and check it lists your job address, and (3) verify workers’ compensation at WCAIS, then keep a copy for your records.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for O'Hara homeowners

With 70% of O’Hara’s housing stock dating to pre-1980, many Allegheny County homes still have galvanized steel supply lines and older drain piping that are nearing the end of their service life. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out—so you may see slower hot water, lower pressure at one fixture, or repeated leaks at threaded fittings. Cast iron drain lines can last a long time, but once roots invade joints or cracks appear, they can start acting like a recurring clog problem.

The decision between targeted repair and partial or full repipe depends on how widespread the damage is and how accessible the affected piping is. If a single leak is isolated in an accessible run, a spot repair can be the most cost-effective approach. But if you’re seeing multiple leaks over a few seasons—especially on galvanized sections—repiping becomes the smarter value because it removes the underlying failure mechanism rather than paying to patch the same system repeatedly.

Here’s where homeowners in O’Hara often make the best decision: if your home shows a pattern of recurring leaks (for example, two or more incidents in 3 years) on galvanized lines, a partial repipe at around $1,500–$900 may be justified as a “stop the bleeding” move. On the other hand, if you’re paying for excavation and replacement in multiple distant locations, it can tip the job toward a full repipe—particularly when you’re already opening walls or dealing with reduced pressure throughout the house.

How to choose a plumbing contractor in O'Hara

Choosing the right plumber in O’Hara is mostly about verifying credentials and getting a quote that matches what your home needs—not what’s easiest to sell. If the job involves sewer scopes, trenchless relines, or diagnostic camera equipment, ask if they are properly trained and certified for that work (including any IICRC-related credentials the company claims).

Next, get 2–3 written, itemized quotes. You want line items that separate labor from materials, explain what will be replaced, and specify whether camera inspection is included. Ask about warranty. For payment, never pay more than 10–15% upfront; hold back payment until the job is complete, cleaned up, and verified. Finally, insist on a written arrival estimate and completion window—emergency dispatch can be faster, but planned work should still come with scheduling clarity.

What causes low water pressure in O'Hara homes?

Low water pressure in O’Hara homes is usually one of three things: a supply-side restriction, an obstruction in the plumbing system, or a fixture/valve issue. In older Allegheny County housing, the most common cause is internal corrosion in older galvanized supply lines. Over decades, mineral buildup and corrosion reduce pipe diameter, which shows up as weak flow at multiple fixtures—often worse at the hot side depending on how the system is arranged.

Another cause can be a partially closed shutoff valve, a failing pressure regulator (if your home has one), or sediment in the water heater that affects hot water flow. If you’ve also noticed recurring leaks or damp spots, the low pressure may be tied to an aging service line or hidden supply piping failure that’s gradually changing system behavior.

Because O’Hara’s older homes are common, it’s worth diagnosing rather than guessing. If the cause is galvanized corrosion across multiple runs, a diagnosis and repipe discussion can become necessary—local budgeting may align with the $1,500–$900 range in scenarios where repipe is recommended after testing, versus smaller repair costs when the issue is localized to a fixture or accessible valve.
