Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued through the Pennsylvania Department of Labor & Industry (verify at dli.pa.gov) and through the PA Plumbing Board. For North Londonderry homeowners, the practical takeaway is simple: ask who is licensed, and make sure the permit—when needed—is pulled by the licensed contractor.

Work that typically DOES require a permit (or inspection coordination) includes: new installation of plumbing fixtures, pipe replacement beyond minor repairs, water heater installation (including changes to venting), sewer line work, and many projects that require a local code officer inspection. Repairs that typically do NOT require a permit include a like-for-like replacement of a failing flapper or supply line, fixing a leaking faucet cartridge, or minor drain clearing when no piping is being replaced.

Lead service lines: Pennsylvania’s Act 2021-41 requires water utilities to replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (from street to house) can still be lead on older North Londonderry properties. Ask your plumber to test and document what they find before replacement planning.

Workers’ compensation: confirm coverage using WCAIS at wcais.pa.gov and request an insurance certificate listing your address. If a contractor lacks coverage, homeowners can face serious liability if an uninsured worker is injured on the property.

  1. Get the plumber’s PA license info and confirm it on DLI/board resources.
  2. Ask whether a permit is required for your exact scope before work begins.
  3. Request a certificate of insurance showing your property address.
  4. Verify workers’ compensation coverage via WCAIS or ask for proof of policy.
  5. Get the permit number (if pulled) and inspection plan in writing.

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for North Londonderry homeowners

With about 47% of North Londonderry’s housing stock built before 1980, many Lebanon County homes still have galvanized steel supply lines or cast-iron drain lines approaching the end of their service life (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024). Galvanized pipe can fail from the inside out: corrosion reduces internal diameter, causes recurring leaks, and eventually leads to low pressure or sudden failures. Cast iron drains often last longer than many expect, but once you see cracks, separation at joints, or recurring clogs that aren’t solved by cleaning, it’s usually time to investigate the sewer system rather than repeating the same spot fix.

Choosing repair versus repipe comes down to system age, how many leaks you’ve had, whether the pipe runs are accessible, and whether you’re seeing signs of corrosion across multiple fixtures. For example, a single leak in an accessible run might justify a spot repair. But if you have recurring leaks in different rooms or branches, you’re often paying to fix the “latest” failure while other segments are failing too.

Here’s a practical dollar example: if you’re quoted around $450–$2500 for a spot repair but you’ve had multiple failures (2+ within a few years) on galvanized lines, the long-term value usually shifts toward partial repipe. Upgrading an affected section can cost in the range of $900–$900 for a targeted partial repipe scenario, and in many cases it stops the cycle of opening walls for the next leak.

Because Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycles stress older piping, even small issues can turn into burst events during cold snaps—so decisions made after one leak can protect you from a much larger emergency later.

Next, get 2–3 quotes that are itemized—not lump sum. A good quote breaks down labor and materials and lists what’s included: locate service, access method, parts, disposal, testing, and cleanup. What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water supply lines coated with zinc, commonly found in older Pennsylvania homes. They can work for years, but they corrode from the inside out, gradually reducing flow and increasing the likelihood of leaks. In North Londonderry and Lebanon County—where many homes are older, with a substantial portion built before 1980—galvanized lines are a frequent cause behind recurring leaks, low pressure, and “it started as one joint” failures. Whether to replace depends on how widespread the corrosion is and whether your leaks are isolated or repeating. If you’ve had a single accessible leak and the rest of the system tests well, a spot repair can be reasonable. But if you’re seeing multiple leaks across different branches, replacement planning often becomes more cost-effective than repeating wall openings. A plumber can recommend spot, partial, or full repipe after evaluating flow, leak history, and pipe condition.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in North Londonderry winters?

Frozen pipe prevention in North Londonderry is mostly about insulation, circulation, and reducing exposure to freeze-thaw stress. Start by locating any plumbing in garages, crawlspaces, basements with drafts, and exterior wall runs—older homes in the area often have piping that’s harder to keep warm. Insulate exposed sections, seal air leaks around penetrations, and consider heat tape only where appropriate and installed safely by a qualified plumber. During deep cold snaps, keep cabinet doors open on interior plumbing to allow warmer air to circulate. If you have long unheated runs, let a faucet trickle to reduce pressure spikes when temperatures drop. Because Pennsylvania winters can rapidly swing temperatures, even pipes that survived last season may burst when conditions change—especially in older construction. If you already had a leak from freeze risk, ask your plumber about addressing insulation gaps or rerouting vulnerable sections before the next cold snap.

What is covered under homeowner's insurance for plumbing in North Londonderry?

Homeowners’ insurance typically covers sudden and accidental water damage from plumbing failures, but it often excludes slow leaks, lack of maintenance, and damage that develops over time. For example, if a pipe bursts due to freeze conditions or an unexpected failure, the resulting water damage may be covered depending on your policy. If the issue is a slow leak that has been present for weeks, insurers may deny coverage or reduce payouts because it can be categorized as maintenance-related. Because North Londonderry homes include many older structures, documenting the timeline matters—photos, repair invoices, and plumber reports can help show the failure was sudden. Also, sewer backup coverage varies widely by policy; it may require optional endorsements. Pennsylvania regulations and permitting requirements don’t automatically control insurance, but ensuring repairs are done by a properly licensed contractor and keeping records supports a smoother claim process.
