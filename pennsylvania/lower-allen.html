What's the difference between a tank and tankless water heater?
Tank water heaters (40-80 gallon) heat and store water continuously — lower upfront cost ($983-$2951) but heat standby water 24/7. Tankless (on-demand) heaters heat water only when you need it — 24-34% more energy efficient, endless hot water, but higher upfront cost and may require gas line upgrades. For most Lower Allen families of 3-4 people, a 50-gallon gas tank heater is sufficient. Larger families or homes with multiple simultaneous showers may benefit from tankless. Permits & regulations in Pennsylvania

In Pennsylvania, plumbing work must be performed by or under the direct supervision of a licensed Master Plumber or Journeyman Plumber. Licenses are issued by the PA Department of Labor & Industry (verify at dli.pa.gov) and the PA Plumbing Board. This matters because it ties your job to required code practices, proper materials, and inspection readiness.

Permits are typically required for work such as: installing new plumbing fixtures (new toilet, sink, shower, etc.), replacing plumbing piping beyond minor repairs, water heater installation or replacement, sewer line work (including spot repairs and relining), and any work that triggers local code-officer inspection. In contrast, common “like-for-like” replacements such as fixing a leaking faucet, replacing a toilet flapper, or swapping an existing wax ring often do not require a permit because there’s no substantial change to the plumbing system.

Lead service lines are also a Pennsylvania concern. Under PA Act 2021-41, water utilities replace lead service lines, but the homeowner-side lateral (the portion from the street to the house) may still contain lead on pre-1986 homes. Ask your plumber to test and document the service line material so the correct replacement plan and notifications can be followed.

Before work starts, confirm insurance and worker protection. Verify workers’ compensation coverage using WCAIS (wcais.pa.gov), and request a certificate of insurance listing your property address. If a contractor is uninsured, homeowners can face liability if an uninsured worker is injured on-site.

Step-by-step for Lower Allen homeowners: (1) check the plumber’s license on the PA licensing registry, (2) request a COI and confirm it includes general liability and workers’ comp, (3) verify workers’ comp in WCAIS, and (4) confirm the specific permit scope in writing (especially for sewer, water heater changes, and any drain/water service alterations).

When to repair vs. repipe — a guide for Lower Allen homeowners

With about 55.7% of Lower Allen’s housing stock dating to pre-1980 (U.S. Census Bureau, ACS 2024), many homes still have plumbing components that are nearing end-of-life. You’ll commonly see galvanized steel supply lines in older builds and, in many basements and older bathrooms, cast iron drain lines that have accumulated decades of scale and corrosion. Galvanized pipe corrodes from the inside out—water pressure drops, sediment builds, and eventually pinhole leaks or bursts can occur. The key question is whether you can fix the problem you see today, or whether repairs will simply lead to the next failure.

Targeted repair usually makes the most sense when a leak is isolated, the pipe is accessible, and the rest of the system shows no signs of chronic corrosion. Repiping—partial or full—becomes smarter when the issue is recurring, when multiple leaks appear close together, or when water pressure is low throughout the house because buildup has effectively reduced pipe diameter. Access is also critical: if plumbing is buried behind finished walls, “patch and pray” can become expensive after repeated openings.

Lower Allen’s freeze-thaw cycles also raise the stakes. Pipes that are already weakened by corrosion are more likely to fail when temperatures swing and water expands in exterior-wall runs, garages, and unfinished basements. That’s why, in some homes, a repair that costs less upfront can become the wrong choice if you’re repeatedly paying for emergency shutoffs and excavation.

Example: if you’re deciding between spot repair and partial repipe for recurring leaks tied to galvanized piping, a spot repair might start near the $350–$1,800 range for an accessible repair, but a pattern (2+ leaks in 3 years) often justifies partial repiping in the $1,400–$900 band—because you’re paying once to stop the systemic corrosion, rather than paying repeatedly.

What causes low water pressure in Lower Allen homes?

Low water pressure in Lower Allen is often caused by restriction or loss of flow somewhere in the system, and in older homes it frequently ties back to pipe corrosion. In Cumberland County, many properties have pre-1980 plumbing components, which can include galvanized supply lines that corrode internally over time. When scale and rust build up, the effective pipe diameter shrinks, leading to weak flow at multiple fixtures—not just one faucet. Another common cause is a partially closed shutoff valve, a failing pressure regulator, or a water-supply issue upstream of the home.

Because Lower Allen experiences freeze-thaw cycles, check whether you’ve had recent cold snaps that could have led to small leaks behind walls or in exterior-wall runs. Those leaks can reduce pressure and waste water, sometimes before they turn into visible flooding. If you’re seeing low pressure throughout the house, the right first step is diagnosis: check fixtures one-by-one, verify the main shutoff and any pressure regulator settings, and have a plumber evaluate suspected galvanized sections. Depending on what’s found, costs can range from a diagnostic visit to more involved work; if the issue is widespread galvanized corrosion, repiping scenarios can approach the higher end of typical repair planning rather than a small fix.

What are galvanized pipes and should I replace them?

Galvanized pipes are steel water-supply pipes with a zinc coating used in many older homes. Over decades, the interior coating can corrode and accumulate sediment, which can reduce water pressure, cause pinhole leaks, and eventually lead to bursts—risks that are heightened during Pennsylvania’s freeze-thaw cycles. In Lower Allen specifically, where a significant portion of the housing stock was built before 1980, galvanized piping is still something many homeowners run into.

Should you replace them? It depends on how the system behaves. If you have a single isolated leak in an accessible section, spot repair may be reasonable—often starting in the $350–$1,800 range for an accessible repair. But if you’re seeing recurring leaks (2+ in 3 years), multiple wet-wall spots, or widespread low pressure throughout the house, replacement becomes more cost-effective than repeated repairs.

For planning, repipe options can scale from partial to full based on how much pipe is affected and what can be accessed. Your plumber should diagnose and explain where corrosion is most likely, and whether a targeted partial repipe can solve the issue. In some scenarios, older galvanized supply lines end up being candidates for full replacement to copper or PEX to stop future failures.

How do I prevent frozen pipes in Lower Allen winters?

Lower Allen winters can be tough on plumbing because of temperature swings. Prevent frozen pipes by reducing exposure: keep cabinet doors open in bathrooms and kitchens on exterior walls, and don’t let hoses or water supply lines sit in unheated garages or poorly insulated crawlspaces without protection. If you have known problem runs—like pipes near exterior walls, garages, or unfinished basements—add insulation to the pipe and, where appropriate, use heat tape rated for water pipes. Maintain a small, steady drip from the coldest fixture during extreme cold, especially overnight when temperatures drop the most.

Before a hard freeze, check that exterior shutoff valves and hose bibs are in good condition and consider letting your plumber assess insulation gaps or drafts around pipe penetrations. If you’ve had slow leaks or recurring pressure loss, fix them quickly—water movement and small leaks can complicate freeze behavior. Also, ensure your home’s shutoff valve is accessible so you can stop water fast if a pipe does freeze and then fails.

Emergency response is more expensive than planned work, so prevention is usually cheaper than an after-hours burst call—emergency burst pipe repairs in this market commonly fall around $1,800–$4,800.
